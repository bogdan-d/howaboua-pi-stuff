# AI Writing Trope Checklist

Use this after the purpose and substance are clear. Diagnose what the prose is doing before reaching for the word lists. No single phrase, punctuation mark, sentence length, or structure proves that writing is AI-generated.

## Structural tells

### Generic throat-clearing

- in today's fast-paced world
- in today's rapidly evolving landscape
- as technology continues to evolve
- now more than ever
- it is no secret that
- when it comes to
- opening with a broad claim the intended reader already accepts

Start with the actual event, claim, question, object, or observation.

### Repetition disguised as structure

- a heading, subtitle, opening paragraph, bullets, and conclusion all making the same claim
- a paragraph politely summarizing the paragraph before it
- generic “why it matters” closers
- “in conclusion” or “in summary” when the ending adds nothing
- announcing the next section instead of moving into it

Remove the duplicate rather than finding a new synonym for it.

### Manufactured momentum

- “Here's the thing:”
- “The best part?”
- “But that's not all.”
- “Why does this matter?”
- “The real kicker?” when nothing kicks
- “Let's dive in.”
- rhetorical questions immediately answered by the writer
- furthermore, moreover, and additionally used as automatic paragraph openers

Keep signposting only when it helps the reader follow a real turn in the argument.

### Fake contrast and symmetry

- not just X, but Y
- more than just X
- it's not X; it's Y
- where X meets Y
- from X to Y
- “whether you're A, B, or C” funnels trying to include everyone
- three-item adjective stacks such as “powerful, flexible, intuitive”
- every section using setup, contrast, and punchline in the same order

Contrast and groups of three are valid. Repeated symmetry is the tell.

### Inflated shape

- turning a small point into many headings and subheadings
- bullets whose items could be one sentence
- every paragraph having the same length and cadence
- one idea stretched into a framework, journey, or manifesto
- numbered lessons that differ only in wording

Match structure to the amount of thought present.

### Sentence complexity without payoff

- several independent ideas joined by commas, semicolons, or em dashes
- nested qualifications that separate subject from verb
- abstract noun chains hiding who did what
- long sentences used to simulate nuance
- short fragments injected only to simulate personality

Prefer one main idea per sentence. Keep a long sentence when its clauses genuinely need to be understood together.

### Feature-to-feeling leaps

- a technical feature followed immediately by “so you can focus on what matters”
- a convenience described as freedom, confidence, or transformation
- an ordinary tool presented as a movement or new era
- “designed to help you” without naming the help

Explain the mechanism or stop after the factual feature.

## Truth and stance failures

### Unsupported certainty

- changing “might help” into “delivers”
- changing “we think” into an objective fact
- generalizing from one example without support
- “experts agree” or “research shows” without a source
- describing an assumption as a decision already made

Editing MUST preserve epistemic status: what is known, believed, inferred, hoped, disputed, or unresolved.

### Invented specificity

- made-up numbers, examples, quotations, users, outcomes, or personal experiences
- claiming a feature or behavior not present in the source
- adding sensory or biographical detail merely to sound human

Use supplied or verified detail. Narrow an unsupported claim. Invent only in explicitly creative work where invention is expected.

### Canned empathy and borrowed authority

- “We understand how frustrating X can be” without evidence or relationship
- “You're not alone” as automatic reassurance
- faux vulnerability followed by a pitch
- speaking for communities, customers, or experts without basis

Name the concrete problem or speak from the writer's actual position.

### Erased caveats

- removing uncertainty because it sounds less polished
- hiding tradeoffs, limitations, incompatibilities, or unfinished work
- replacing a precise exception with a clean universal claim

Caveats can be the most trustworthy and human part of the prose. Keep them readable, not invisible.

## Phrase blacklist

These are review alarms, not automatic deletions.

### Hard-cut boilerplate

- in today's fast-paced world
- in the ever-evolving world or landscape of
- it is important to note
- it is worth noting
- at the end of the day
- a testament to
- plays a crucial or pivotal role
- the future of X
- game-changing, revolutionary, or transformative without evidence
- I hope this email finds you well
- I wanted to reach out
- please do not hesitate to

### High-risk verbs

- unlock
- unleash
- elevate
- empower
- streamline
- transform
- enhance
- leverage when “use” works
- delve
- navigate the complexities of
- harness
- supercharge
- underscore
- foster
- facilitate when a concrete verb exists

### High-risk adjectives

- seamless
- robust
- powerful
- intuitive
- innovative
- cutting-edge
- dynamic
- comprehensive
- effortless
- scalable when no scaling behavior is named
- meticulous
- pivotal
- crucial
- vibrant

### Abstraction fog

- landscape
- ecosystem
- journey
- solution
- experience
- platform
- capabilities
- potential
- productivity
- workflow when the work is never named
- realm
- tapestry
- intersection when used as “at the intersection of X and Y”

### Generic product language

- built for modern teams
- all-in-one solution
- move faster than ever
- designed to help you
- without compromising on quality
- take X to the next level
- ready to transform your workflow?
- get started today when a more exact action is available

## Genre-specific risks

### Technical writing and documentation

- removing repetition that prevents mistakes
- replacing exact terminology with conversational synonyms
- turning known limitations into benefits
- adding personality inside warnings, errors, or safety instructions
- simplifying until prerequisites or failure conditions disappear

### Essays and articles

- forcing every paragraph into claim, evidence, takeaway
- replacing transitions with disconnected punchy fragments
- flattening ambiguity or tension into a clean lesson
- ending with a universal moral the argument did not earn

### Emails and messages

- ceremonial openings before the reason for writing
- excessive gratitude or apology
- burying the actual question
- a warm paragraph followed by a redundant formal summary

### Product and marketing writing

- value claims with no mechanism or evidence
- pretending the product serves every audience
- inflated stakes for ordinary features
- several interchangeable adjectives before the product is explained
- vague calls to action

### Social posts

- launch-thread cadence on an ordinary observation
- numbered lessons with matching rhythm
- “This changed everything” without a before and after
- fake rebellion, founder-guru certainty, or manufactured vulnerability

### Bios and personal writing

- self-description made entirely of adjectives
- “passionate about” instead of evidence of what the person does
- polishing away regional language, odd phrasing, humour, or restraint
- adding hobbies, motives, or experiences that were never supplied

## Better moves

### Lead with the thing

Instead of:

> In today's rapidly evolving AI landscape, adaptability is no longer optional.

Use the specific observation:

> Yesterday's clever AI trick becomes next month's plumbing.

### Name the work

Instead of:

> Streamline your development workflow.

Use:

> Keep the issue, diff, terminal, and review notes in one place.

### Replace promise with mechanism

Instead of:

> Empowers developers to collaborate with agents.

Use:

> Gives agents terminal access, git context, and the current task instructions.

### Narrow the claim

Instead of:

> Eliminates context switching.

Use:

> Keeps the terminal and review notes beside the session.

### Preserve uncertainty

Instead of:

> This architecture scales cleanly.

Use, when that is all the evidence supports:

> This should remove the current bottleneck. We have not tested it at larger scale yet.

### Split the sentence

Instead of stacking setup, qualification, conclusion, and benefit into one sentence, give the main claim its own sentence. Attach only the caveat needed to interpret it.

### Use a plain action

Prefer `Install`, `Download`, `View source`, `Reply`, or `Send the file` over a motivational call to action.

### Keep the writer's phrase

If the source contains one odd but clear phrase, preserve it. Automated polishing often removes the only line that could belong to that writer.

## Do not overcorrect

- Do not ban em dashes, semicolons, adjectives, transitions, long sentences, or groups of three.
- Do not make every sentence short and blunt.
- Do not inject slang, swearing, jokes, fragments, or typos as proof of humanity.
- Do not flatten every writer into the same dry product voice.
- Do not remove formal conventions that serve legal, medical, academic, regulatory, or safety writing.
- Do not optimize for AI detectors. Improve meaning, truthfulness, rhythm, and voice.

The useful test is not “Does this look human?” It is: could this writing belong to this author, subject, audience, and moment—or could it be pasted onto fifty unrelated things unchanged?
